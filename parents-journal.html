Processing the news: how this changes our daily life, our costs, and our family

The road ahead

Changes to Our Daily Routine

I won't pretend this doesn't change everything — because it does. A child with Patau Syndrome, depending on how the condition affects them, may require around-the-clock monitoring, daily therapies, and frequent specialist visits, especially in the first year of life. My spouse and I have started talking through what that actually looks like week to week.

Accommodations We Need to Make

We've started making a list of what our home and life need to look like:

  • Medical equipment space — feeding pumps, oxygen equipment, baby monitors all need somewhere to live in our home
  • Work adjustments — one of us will likely need to reduce hours or go part-time, at least initially
  • A specialist rotation — cardiologist, neurologist, ophthalmologist, ENT, and feeding therapist means a lot of appointments to coordinate
  • Early intervention therapies — physical, occupational, and speech therapy typically start in the first months of life
  • Seizure safety — padding, monitoring, avoiding situations that could be dangerous during a seizure event

Estimated Medical Costs

I've been doing the research, and the financial reality is something we need to face directly. These are rough estimates — every child's situation is different — but they give us a sense of what we're planning for.

$100K+ Avg. NICU stay, complex newborn
$20K–80K Heart surgery (varies widely)
$5K–15K/yr Ongoing therapies & specialists

We've been looking into Medicaid waivers for medically complex children, SSI disability income, and nonprofit grant programs. We're also checking whether our insurance plan has a NICU max-out-of-pocket.

The Emotional Weight

My spouse and I have been having a lot of conversations in the dark lately — the kind where neither of you is really ready to say what you're actually thinking. I felt grief first. Then guilt. Then love, in a way I didn't expect — fierce and protective.

We've talked about what this means for our relationship, for any siblings our child might have, for our parents who are already asking questions. We're considering counseling — not because we're falling apart, but because we want to handle this the right way together.

Will We Need to Move?

One of the first things I researched after the diagnosis was whether our city has what our child would need. The answer isn't straightforward. Pediatric cardiac surgery, a Level IV NICU, and a strong early intervention program all need to be within reach.

Research what medical infrastructure exists in YOUR actual city/state. Does your area have a children's hospital with a NICU? A pediatric cardiology program? Name specific facilities you found. Would you need to travel or relocate? Be specific.

How Will Our Child Be Treated?

This is the question that keeps me up more than the medical stuff. Children with Patau Syndrome who survive into childhood often look and develop differently from their peers. I think about the stares in public. The questions from other kids. Whether they'll find friends.

We've started thinking about what advocacy looks like from day one — at the pediatrician's office, at the school meeting, at the birthday party where a kid says something unkind.

Reflect on the social and emotional experience of your child. Will they attend special education programs? How will you talk to other children about their sibling? How do you plan to advocate for your child's dignity and inclusion? Think about their perspective too, not just yours.

Heredity — What This Means for Future Pregnancies

After everything we learned from the genetic counselor, we know our recurrence risk depends on which form of Trisomy 13 our child has. In most cases, the risk is low but not zero — and it goes up with maternal age.

We've agreed that if we have more children, we'll pursue prenatal genetic testing early. Knowing ahead of time doesn't change how much we'll love that child — but it gives us time to prepare.
