What Is Patau Syndrome?

Patau Syndrome — also called Trisomy 13 — is a chromosomal condition in which a person is born with three copies of chromosome 13 instead of the usual two. This extra genetic material interferes with normal fetal development, affecting nearly every organ system. It was first described by physician Klaus Patau in 1960.

Symptoms

Patau Syndrome presents with a wide range of physical findings, many visible at birth or during prenatal imaging. Common features include:

  • Cleft lip and/or cleft palate
  • Extra fingers or toes (polydactyly)
  • Congenital heart defects — present in up to 80% of cases
  • Brain malformations, including holoprosencephaly (the brain fails to divide into two hemispheres)
  • Small or absent eyes (microphthalmia or anophthalmia)
  • Kidney and internal organ abnormalities
  • Scalp skin defects (aplasia cutis)
  • Low birth weight and severe intellectual disability in those who survive infancy

Treatment Options

Is there a cure?

There is no cure for Patau Syndrome. Because the extra chromosome is present in every cell of the body, it cannot be surgically removed or medically corrected. Treatment is entirely focused on managing individual symptoms and improving quality of life.

Surgical options

Depending on which organs are affected, surgery may be recommended for cleft lip and palate repair, heart defect correction, polydactyly (extra digit removal), or intestinal malformations. However, surgical decisions are highly individualized and often depend on the overall severity of the condition.

Medical management

  • Anti-seizure medications — seizures are common; anticonvulsants help manage them
  • Heart medications — depending on the cardiac defect present
  • Feeding support — many infants require a nasogastric (NG) tube or a surgically placed gastrostomy tube (G-tube) due to feeding difficulties
  • Respiratory support — supplemental oxygen or mechanical ventilation may be required

Palliative and comfort care

Some families, in close discussion with their medical team, choose comfort-focused care — prioritizing the child's comfort and quality of life over aggressive interventions, particularly when malformations are very severe. This is a deeply personal decision and one our care team will fully support either way.

Current Research & Progress Toward a Cure

As of now, no cure for Trisomy 13 exists. Researchers continue to improve surgical outcomes and neonatal intensive care protocols, which have extended survival times over recent decades. Some experimental research is exploring chromosomal silencing techniques — similar to approaches being studied for Trisomy 21 (Down Syndrome) — but these remain in early stages and are not yet clinical treatments.

Assistive Technologies & Products

For children with Patau Syndrome who survive infancy, several technologies help support daily functioning:

  • AAC devices — Augmentative and Alternative Communication tools for nonverbal children (e.g., Tobii Dynavox, PRC Accent)
  • Feeding equipment — Specialized bottles, NG tube kits, G-tube systems
  • Hearing aids — for children with hearing loss
  • Visual aids — corrective lenses, magnifiers, or low-vision tools
  • Seizure monitoring — wearable alert devices (e.g., Empatica Embrace)
  • Adaptive mobility — custom strollers, orthotics, or wheelchairs
